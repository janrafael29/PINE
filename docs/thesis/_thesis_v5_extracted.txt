ABSTRACT
PINYA-PIC is a machine learning-driven mobile application designed to address the challenges pineapple farmers in Polomolok, South Cotabato face due to mealybug infestations. Mealybugs (Dysmicoccus brevipes) harm crops by reducing photosynthesis and spreading diseases like mealybug wilt, causing significant economic losses. Current pest management relies on inefficient manual inspections that are time-consuming, labor-intensive, and often inadequate to address the rapid spread of infestations.
This study developed PINYA-PIC as an Android-based, machine learning-driven mobile application for assisting pineapple farmers in documenting and reviewing mealybug infestations in Polomolok, South Cotabato. The application combines on-device image analysis, geotagging, local field-boundary checking, saved-image history, and cloud synchronization to support field monitoring in environments where internet connectivity may be unreliable.
The developed application follows a local-first architecture. Captured results are stored in SQLite before cloud upload, while Supabase provides authentication, field metadata, detection storage, and account-linked restoration for records that have already synchronized.
Major implemented features include field creation and editing, saved-images gallery, 7-day dashboard analytics, detections map with severity-based visualization, device-unlock protection, English/Filipino language switching, online-required prompts for internet-dependent actions, and feedback submission through either email or an in-app web form. Geofencing in the system is implemented as local point-in-polygon validation of field boundaries rather than operating-system background geofence triggers.
The detection workflow is still-image based rather than a live camera stream. Users capture a photo or choose one from the gallery, after which a bundled TensorFlow Lite model based on the Ultralytics YOLO26n architecture is executed on-device with EXIF-aware preprocessing, coordinate remapping, and Dart-side Non-Maximum Suppression.
Earlier stages of the project also documented YOLO11-based experimentation, while later development referenced a newer YOLO26n training pipeline. Accordingly, the deployed system is described in this manuscript as using a YOLO26n (Ultralytics YOLO26 Nano) TensorFlow Lite model. PINYA-PIC contributes to Sustainable Development Goals 2 (Zero Hunger) and 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production) by supporting earlier pest documentation, more targeted interventions, and better spatial awareness of infestation patterns.
Keywords: pineapple farming, mealybug infestation, machine learning, YOLO26n, YOLO26, TensorFlow Lite object detection, geotagging, precision agriculture, local-first architecture, Supabase, TensorFlow Lite, System Usability Scale

CHAPTER I
INTRODUCTION
1.0  Background of the Study
Pineapple farming is a vital economic activity in tropical and subtropical regions, providing nutritional benefits and economic opportunities. The growing demand for pineapple consumption and export underscores its significance (Hossain, M. F., 2015). The Philippines is the third-largest pineapple producer globally, with major production centers in Mindanao, particularly in Bukidnon, South Cotabato, and Davao del Norte. In South Cotabato, Polomolok serves as the heart of pineapple production, hosting large-scale plantations and thousands of smallholder farmers who depend on pineapple cultivation for their livelihoods.
However, this industry faces persistent challenges from mealybug wilt, a destructive disease caused by a parasitic complex involving Pineapple Mealybug Wilt-associated Viruses (PMWaVs) and mealybugs (Dysmicoccus brevipes), which act as vectors for these viruses (N’Guessan, L., et al. 2024). Adult pineapple mealybugs are small, approximately 1 mm wide, with a fuzzy appearance due to white wax and a pinkish hue underneath. They feed on the sap of pineapple plants, reducing photosynthesis and growth, and causing stunted growth, poor fruit quality, and, in severe cases, plant death. Their feeding also results in honeydew excretion, promoting sooty mold development and virus transmission (Canario, D. V. P., et al. 2017).
In Polomolok, South Cotabato, mealybug wilt disease presents a significant challenge for pineapple plantations. Local farmers, during interviews in Landan, reported that infestations not only lower crop productivity but also increase pest management costs. Current practices depend heavily on manual inspection, which is time-consuming, labor-intensive, and often inadequate to address the rapid spread of infestations. Farmers typically conduct visual inspections of their fields, searching for visible signs of mealybugs or the characteristic symptoms of wilt disease. This approach has inherent limitations: time consumption, delayed detection, inaccurate assessment, and inefficient pesticide application.
Advancements in machine learning offer innovative solutions to pest management in agriculture. By applying computer vision, machine learning models such as YOLO can analyze crop images, with this study specifically deploying a YOLO26n-based model optimized for mobile inference. When integrated with geotagging and geofencing, these technologies provide precise data on infestation locations and high-risk areas, enabling farmers to implement targeted and efficient pest control measures. This approach aligns with precision agriculture goals, optimizing resource use and promoting sustainable farming practices (Bendre et al., 2021).
To address these challenges, PINYA-PIC, a machine learning-driven mobile application, has been developed to detect mealybug infestations in pineapple plantations. By integrating machine learning, geotagging, and geofencing, PINYA-PIC provides a cost-effective and user-friendly solution for farmers. It identifies mealybug presence, assesses infestation density, and provides actionable insights. PINYA-PIC contributes primarily to Sustainable Development Goals 2 (Zero Hunger) and 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production), with additional support for SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure) and SDG 15 (Life on Land), by automating pest detection and delivering spatial data to enhance infestation management. This helps reduce economic losses, promote sustainable farming practices, and support the long-term productivity of pineapple farming.
The deployed system performs detection using still images captured through the mobile device camera, with processing handled locally through a TensorFlow Lite model to support offline functionality.
1.1 Objectives of the Study
1.1.1 General Objective
The objective of this research is to develop a mobile application that integrates machine learning–driven mealybug detection using a YOLO26n-based model with geotagging and geofencing capabilities to enhance pest management in pineapple plantations. The system aims to provide insights into mealybug presence and population density, support targeted pest control efforts, and visualize historical data, in order to improve the sustainability and productivity of pineapple farming in Polomolok, South Cotabato.
Specific Objectives
Develop and integrate a YOLO26n-based model deployed in TensorFlow Lite format for on-device detection of mealybugs, including performance evaluation through precision, recall, and mAP metrics.
Develop an Android mobile application following a local-first architecture that integrates SQLite for local storage and Supabase for cloud synchronization.
Implement field management features that allow users to create, edit, and maintain field data, including preview images and boundary mapping.
Design and implement visualization features such as a 7-day detection trend chart, infestation statistics, and a map-based severity visualization.
Implement geospatial functionality including GPS acquisition, EXIF metadata handling, manual map pinning, and local point-in-polygon geofencing.
Develop offline-capable workflows with synchronization mechanisms for uploading data when connectivity is available.
Integrate additional usability and reliability features including feedback mechanisms, device unlock, saved-image gallery, and optional split-per-ABI packaging support.
Evaluate the usability of the application using the System Usability Scale (SUS) and task-based testing.

1.3 Scope and Limitation
This study focuses on the design, implementation, and evaluation of a mobile application for documenting and reviewing mealybug infestations in pineapple farms. Its scope covers four major areas: on-device image inference, Android mobile application development, local-first data management with cloud synchronization, and geospatial visualization of saved detections. The system applies a default confidence threshold of 0.20 (20%) as the minimum model score required to retain a detection during inference.
For model integration, the mobile application uses a bundled TensorFlow Lite detector from the YOLO26n and performs image preprocessing, EXIF orientation correction, coordinate remapping, confidence filtering, and Dart-side Non-Maximum Suppression on-device.
Earlier development stages explored YOLO11 pipelines; however, the deployed system uses a YOLO26n model exported to TensorFlow Lite. Detection is performed on still images captured by the user and does not involve continuous real-time video processing.
For mobile application development, the implemented system is field-focused rather than plot-focused. It supports account creation and sign-in, onboarding, field creation and editing, boundary mapping, captured-image review, dashboard analytics, detections map visualization, settings, profile management, feedback submission, saved-images gallery, and account-linked restoration of uploaded detections. The application is implemented in Flutter for Android and uses SQLite for local persistence with Supabase for cloud synchronization, authentication, and storage.
For local data handling, SQLite stores land boundaries, captured photos, upload queue entries, cached field metadata, and a legacy local detection table. New detections are written locally first, then uploaded to Supabase when connectivity and authentication are available. Restoration after reinstall is limited to detections that were previously uploaded; restored records may rely on remote image URLs rather than restored local files.
For geospatial support, the application records coordinates from current GPS, last-known GPS when necessary, EXIF metadata when available, or user-selected map pins. Geofencing is implemented as local point-in-polygon validation against stored field boundaries.
The application also includes network-aware dialogs, device unlock, English/Filipino language switching, and a severity-based map view for uploaded detections.
Usability evaluation in this manuscript is based on the recorded SUS assessment and qualitative feedback gathered from farmers in Polomolok, South Cotabato.
Despite the comprehensive scope, the study acknowledges several limitations:
Geographic scope: The research is confined to pineapple plantations in Polomolok, South Cotabato. Although the application may be adaptable to other regions, the usability evaluation and feature validation were conducted exclusively with farmers from this area; variations in mealybug populations, farming practices, and technology infrastructure elsewhere may affect generalizability.
Dataset dependency: The model’s detection accuracy relies on the quality and diversity of the Roboflow dataset. While the dataset was validated in collaboration with the Department of Agriculture, real world variations in mealybug life stages, lighting conditions, and image quality may not be fully captured, and ongoing model refinement may be required as new data becomes available.
Hardware constraints: Older devices with limited processing power or camera quality may exhibit slower inference times or reduced detection accuracy. Although the offline first architecture mitigates connectivity issues, extremely low end devices may still experience performance limitations.
GPS reliability: Location acquisition depends on device hardware, permissions, and satellite availability. The system avoids invalid placeholder coordinates, but it may fall back to last-known location when a fresh fix is unavailable.
Cloud restoration limitation: Only detections that have been successfully uploaded to Supabase can be restored after reinstallation. Purely offline captures that never synchronized remain local and are lost when the application is uninstalled. Additionally, per-detection confidence JSON is not stored on the server; therefore, cloud-restored rows show only the total bug count and overall confidence, not individual bounding-box confidence labels.
Validation metrics caveat: The reported mAP values (0.526, 0.247) are from the validation split during training; no independent test set evaluation was performed, so these numbers may not represent real world performance on unseen data.
Feedback endpoint dependency: The in-app feedback form depends on a web endpoint and internet connectivity; if the endpoint changes or becomes unavailable, users must fall back to the email-based feedback option.
Filipino translations: Some disease descriptions may have incomplete translations, requiring ongoing refinement.
Single pest detection: The model detects only mealybugs. Expanding to other pests would require additional training data and model refinement.
User training: Although the usability evaluation indicates the system is accessible to users with varying technical backgrounds, farmers with minimal smartphone experience could benefit from additional guidance to fully utilize all features.
1.4 Definition of Terms
To provide clarity and a better understanding of the study, the following terms are defined operationally:
Account Linked Gallery – The feature that, after login, restores previously uploaded detection records from Supabase into the local SQLite gallery so they remain visible after reinstalling the application. Restored rows may be backed by remote image URLs rather than restored local files.
Bounded Cache – Not applicable; the system uses SQLite and Supabase with offline persistence, but the principle of limiting storage usage is applied through queue management.
Confidence Score – A numerical value between 0 and 1 indicating the model’s certainty that a detected object is a mealybug. Higher scores indicate greater confidence in the detection. In the application, this value is presented to users as a percentage (0%–100%) for easier interpretation.
Detection Pipeline – The sequence of operations from image capture to result display, including preprocessing, model inference, bounding box generation, and data persistence.
Device Unlock – A security feature that uses the device’s built-in biometric (fingerprint/face) or PIN to authenticate the user after login, providing an extra layer of protection without requiring re-entry of the application password.
Geofencing – In this study, geofencing refers to local point-in-polygon validation of a captured or selected image’s coordinates against stored field-boundary polygons. It is used for spatial checking and visualization, not operating-system background entry/exit triggers.
Geotagging – The process of appending geographic coordinates to media based on the location of a mobile device, enabling precise tracking of infestation locations.
Inference – The process of running a trained machine learning model on new input data to generate predictions. In this study, inference refers to the bundled YOLO26n-based TensorFlow Lite model processing a still image to detect mealybugs.
Inference Time – The time required for the model to process an image and return detection results, measured in milliseconds.
Field Edit Action – The field-edit workflow in the application is accessed through edit buttons or menu actions on field cards and field detail views, allowing users to update field metadata and preview images without recreating the record.
Network Reachability – A utility that distinguishes between usable network connectivity for UI actions and stricter host reachability checks for background synchronization.
Non Maximum Suppression (NMS) – A post-processing technique used in object detection to eliminate duplicate bounding boxes for the same object, keeping only the most confident detection.
Offline First Architecture – A software design approach where the application prioritizes local storage and functionality when offline, with data synchronized to cloud services when connectivity is restored. In this study, captured-photo history and pending uploads are written locally first, then synchronized to Supabase when conditions allow.
Online Required Dialog – A modal dialog that appears when a user attempts an action requiring internet connectivity while the device is offline; it explains the need and offers to retry when connectivity is restored.
Severity Score – A derived metric that combines bug count and confidence into a single value between 0 and 1, used to color code map markers and guide intervention priority. It is computed as s=1-e^(-b⋅(c/100)/8), where b is the bug count and c is the confidence percentage.
Split APK – A build output where the Android package is split by CPU architecture (e.g., arm64 v8a, armeabi v7a), reducing the download size for each device. This is implemented as an optional configuration during release builds and is not enabled by default in all application packages.
Sync Queue – A database table storing pending upload operations that need to be synchronized with the cloud when connectivity is restored. In this study, the upload_queue table tracks pending uploads, retry attempts, field association hints, and last error messages.
System Usability Scale (SUS) – A standardized questionnaire used to evaluate the perceived usability of a system, consisting of ten items scored on a five-point Likert scale, yielding a single score between 0 and 100.
TensorFlow Lite (TFLite) – Google’s lightweight machine learning framework for mobile and embedded devices, enabling on-device inference with optimized performance and reduced model size.
YOLO (You Only Look Once) – A family of object detection systems that process images in a single forward pass to predict bounding boxes and class probabilities. In this study, the deployed application uses a YOLO26n model converted to TensorFlow Lite for mobile inference.
CHAPTER II
REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE AND TECHNOLOGIES
This chapter reviews literature and technologies relevant to the detection of mealybug infestations in pineapple plants. It includes information from scholarly articles, books, and online resources that support the development of a smartphone-based mealybug detection system for pineapple plantations, utilizing machine learning techniques.
This chapter discusses previous research and existing technologies that contribute to the understanding of mealybug detection, pest management, and the integration of geotagging and geofencing in agricultural systems. It also justifies the proposed system by highlighting its potential impact on improving pest control efficiency and supporting sustainable farming practices in pineapple plantations.
2.0 Related Literature
2.0.1 Machine Learning in Pest Detection
Machine learning has revolutionized pest detection in agriculture, enabling more accurate and efficient interventions. Yulita et al. (2023) demonstrated the potential of mobile applications leveraging Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), achieving an impressive 93.78% accuracy in pest detection using the GoogleNet model.
Their work highlights the feasibility of deploying deep learning models on mobile devices for field-ready pest identification, a foundation that PINYA-PIC builds upon through on-device TensorFlow Lite inference.
Electronic nose (E-Nose) technology has been explored for detecting mealybug infestations in crops. Hazarika et al. (2023) investigated the use of an E-Nose system to detect mealybug infestation in Khasi Mandarin orange plants. By analyzing volatile organic compounds (VOCs) emitted by both infested and healthy plants, they achieved classification accuracies of 95.66% and 96.70% using support vector machine and random forest classifiers, respectively.
This study highlights the potential of alternative sensing technologies for early pest detection, though PINYA-PIC focuses on visual detection which is more accessible via smartphone cameras.
Shilaskar, S., et al. (2023) focused on custard apple plants employing vision-based detection methods using machine learning algorithms to identify mealybug infections. Their research demonstrated the feasibility of automated detection systems in identifying pest infestations, thereby aiding in timely intervention and management. This aligns with the core objective of PINYA-PIC to provide visual pest detection.
2.0.2 Deep Learning Models for Object Detection
Deep learning-based detectors have been developed for identifying insect pests in crops. Li et al. (2022) developed an object recognition system using the YOLOv5 model for detecting and classifying crop-damaging insect pests. The system demonstrated high accuracy and efficiency, highlighting the potential of deep learning models in pest detection applications. This line of work supports the use of a compact YOLO26n detector for mobile agricultural inference.
Chen et al. (2021) developed an AI-based pest detection system utilizing deep learning models such as Faster R-CNN, SSD, and YOLO v4 to identify scale pests in images. Their study highlighted the effectiveness of YOLO-style detectors for mealybug-related image analysis, which is consistent with the detector family used in PINYA-PIC’s mobile workflow.
Esgario et al. (2022) developed an application to assist farmers in identifying diseases and pests in coffee leaves using deep learning techniques. This application aids in the timely detection and management of pests, thereby reducing crop losses. The success of such applications validates the approach of using mobile deep learning for agricultural pest management.
2.0.3 YOLO Architecture Evolution
YOLOv8 is often chosen over other object detection models due to its superior speed and accuracy, making it particularly suitable for real-time applications. Studies, such as Hussain (2024) and Terven and Cordova-Esparza (2023), provide comprehensive analyses of YOLO’s evolution and highlight the advancements in YOLOv8 that enhance its efficiency and precision compared to earlier versions and other detection models.
These improvements solidify YOLO’s position as a leading choice for high-performance object detection in various fields.
More recent work by Sharma et al. (2024) compared multiple YOLO variants and Faster R-CNN for detection of multiple weed species. Their results show that newer YOLO generations can offer strong accuracy-speed trade-offs, supporting the use of a YOLO26n detector for agricultural mobile deployment. These advancements support the selection of a compact YOLO26n model in this study, balancing detection accuracy with computational efficiency for mobile deployment.
2.0.4 Geospatial Technologies in Agriculture
Geotagging and geofencing technologies have been effectively applied in agriculture to enhance monitoring and management practices. Soni et al. (2021) proposed a mobile application that captures photos of agricultural samples and records their latitude and longitude, enabling geotagging of these images.
This approach facilitates precise tracking and analysis of agricultural data. PINYA-PIC extends this by integrating geofencing to verify whether captured images fall within registered land boundaries, adding a layer of verification for data accuracy.
Yang (2020) discussed remote sensing and precision agriculture technologies for crop disease detection and management, highlighting the importance of spatial data in informing pest control decisions.
The combination of machine learning with geospatial data allows farmers to not only detect pests but also understand their spatial distribution, enabling targeted interventions.
2.0.5 Offline-First Mobile Architectures
Chen et al. (2024) examined offline-first architectures for mobile applications in resource-constrained environments, demonstrating that local database storage with queue-based synchronization provides reliable operation in areas with limited connectivity.
Their work shows that SQLite can serve as an authoritative store, with background sync ensuring eventual consistency with cloud databases. PINYA-PIC implements this pattern using SQLite for local storage and Supabase for cloud sync, with an upload_queue table to manage pending operations.
The offline-first approach is particularly relevant for agricultural applications in developing regions where internet connectivity is often unreliable. By ensuring core functionality works without network access, applications can maintain usability even in remote field conditions.
In PINYA-PIC, network reachability utilities further enhance this by distinguishing between Wi Fi association and real internet connectivity, preventing false positive online states.
2.0.6 Mobile Usability in Agricultural Contexts
Brooke (1986) established the System Usability Scale, while Sauro (2011) provided widely-used benchmarks and best practices for this reliable tool for evaluating perceived usability across a wide range of systems. The scale’s simplicity and robustness make it ideal for gathering feedback from users with varying technical experience, including farmers in agricultural settings.
The average SUS score of 68 serves as a benchmark; scores above 70 are considered good, while scores above 80 are excellent.
Wang et al. (2025) reviewed visualization techniques for agricultural data, emphasizing the importance of intuitive chart designs for users with limited numeracy. Simple, visually appealing charts with clear indicators (such as peak highlighting) can significantly improve comprehension of complex data like infestation trends.
2.0.7 Synthesis of Related Literature
The integration of machine learning into agriculture has revolutionized pest detection, enhancing both efficiency and accuracy. Studies like Yulita et al. (2023) demonstrated the potential of mobile applications leveraging CNN models, achieving high accuracy in pest detection.
Deep learning has further propelled advancements in agricultural pest detection, with Li et al. (2022) developing YOLOv5-based systems for identifying insect pests, and Chen et al. (2021) achieving perfect accuracy with YOLO v4 for mealybug detection.
The evolution of YOLO architectures continues across newer releases, offering improvements in accuracy, efficiency, and deployment flexibility. Comparative studies by Sharma et al. (2024) and comprehensive analyses by Hussain (2024) and Terven and Cordova-Esparza (2023) highlight the broader YOLO family’s suitability for mobile-oriented object detection.
Geospatial technologies have enhanced pest monitoring and management in agriculture. Soni et al. (2021) demonstrated how geotagging in mobile applications can improve the tracking and analysis of agricultural data. Offline-first architectures, as discussed by Chen et al. (2024), provide reliable operation in areas with limited connectivity, essential for agricultural applications in rural settings where internet access may be inconsistent.
Mobile usability research, particularly the SUS framework, provides a validated method for assessing user satisfaction. The combination of these technologies and evaluation methods forms the foundation for PINYA-PIC, which aims to deliver a usable, effective, and reliable pest detection solution for pineapple farmers.
However, most existing studies focus on either detection accuracy or system deployment, with limited emphasis on integrated mobile systems that combine offline-first operation, geospatial validation, and real-world usability evaluation, which this study addresses.

METHODOLOGY
3.0 Modified Waterfall Software Development Methodology
The study employed a Modified Waterfall Software Development Methodology, which combines the structured, sequential approach of the traditional waterfall model with flexibility for iterative refinement within phases. This methodology was selected for its suitability to the project’s characteristics: clearly defined requirements, a systematic development process, and the need for well-documented milestones. The phased approach ensures that each stage produces concrete deliverables that serve as the foundation for subsequent work, while the “modified” aspect allows controlled backtracking when necessary—particularly valuable for refining the machine learning model based on testing feedback.
The methodology was implemented in six phases. The Requirements Gathering phase (October 2024 – February 2025) involved interviewing pineapple farmers in Polomolok, reviewing current pest-monitoring practices, and identifying both functional and non-functional requirements for an agricultural mobile application. The System Design phase (February 2025 – September 2025) focused on drafting user flows, interface prototypes, database structures, and the interaction between local storage, cloud synchronization, and map-based visualization. Deliverables included use case diagrams, activity diagrams, interface mock-ups, and database designs for both SQLite and Supabase.
During the Implementation phase (May 2025 – February 2026), the Android Flutter application was built and integrated with a bundled TensorFlow Lite detector from the YOLO26n, local SQLite persistence, and Supabase synchronization. The implemented system includes onboarding, account authentication, saved-image history, detections map, severity visualization, feedback submission, device unlock, Filipino language support, and account-linked restoration for already uploaded detections. The application also implements coordinate remapping, EXIF-aware preprocessing, and Dart-side Non-Maximum Suppression for on-device inference.
The Testing phase (January 2026 – April 2026) included functional testing, usability testing, offline/local-first testing, and security verification. The Deployment phase (April 2026 – June 2026) focused on Android packaging, release configuration, Supabase setup, and team handoff documentation. Maintenance remains ongoing and includes feature refinement, model updates, and corrections based on user feedback and field use.
3.1 Requirements Gathering
The requirements phase established a clear understanding between the development team and the target users regarding the software’s functionality and features. It began with identifying the challenges farmers face in detecting and managing mealybug infestations. Fifteen independent pineapple farmers from Polomolok, South Cotabato, were selected for pilot interviews based on inclusion parameters: a minimum of five years of farming experience, direct experience with mealybug infestations, willingness to provide insights on current pest management practices, and varied levels of technology experience to capture diverse perspectives.
The interview protocol was designed to gather both quantitative and qualitative data. Questions covered pest experience and management, current detection methods, challenges encountered, technology acceptance, feature preferences, and usability expectations. The interview questions are detailed in the original thesis, covering topics such as whether farmers had experienced mealybug infestations, how they currently manage them, their willingness to use a mobile application, desired features (e.g., real time maps, historical data, offline functionality), and the importance of confidence levels, pest density reports, and field editing capabilities. From these interviews, a detailed list of functional and non functional requirements was derived.
Functional requirements specified that the system must allow users to create accounts, log in, manage fields, define or edit field boundaries, capture or select photos, run on-device mealybug detection, save detections locally, synchronize eligible records to Supabase, view saved-image history, review recent analytics, submit feedback, and visualize detections on a map. Additional requirements included network-aware prompts for online-only actions, optional device unlock, English/Filipino language switching, and restoration of previously uploaded detections after reinstall. Non-functional requirements emphasized Android usability, local-first operation, bounded storage through queue-based synchronization, and responsive on-device inference.
3.2 Design
3.2.1 System Design Specification
The system design specification defined the architecture, components, and interfaces of the PINYA-PIC mobile application. The implemented system follows a layered Flutter architecture with separation between presentation, core state/configuration, services, utilities, and persistence. The presentation layer contains screens for onboarding, authentication, dashboard navigation, field management, maps, captured-photo review, profile management, settings, and feedback. The core layer provides dependency injection, global app state, theme handling, Supabase initialization, administrator-session helpers, and persistent security preferences. The data layer spans local SQLite tables and Supabase tables/storage, while service classes encapsulate inference, synchronization, image storage, geolocation, geofence checks, and profile/detection operations.
Key design principles include local-first persistence, field-centered data modeling, reusable service classes, reactive dashboard updates, network-aware user-interface behavior, and account-linked continuity of uploaded records across reinstalls.
This structured design ensures modularity, maintainability, and scalability of the system while supporting offline-first operation and real-time user interaction.
3.2.2 Conceptual Framework
The conceptual framework of the PINYA-PIC system begins with the user launching the mobile application and creating or selecting a field context. The user then captures a still image or selects one from the gallery, after which the on-device detector analyzes the image for mealybugs. The application stores the result locally, associates it with available spatial information, and presents the output in the saved-images gallery, dashboard, and detections map. Through this flow, users can build a historical record of infestations and use both temporal and spatial views to support follow-up action.
The framework follows an input–process–output (IPO) structure, where captured images and field data serve as inputs, machine learning inference and geospatial processing act as the core processes, and detection results, analytics, and map visualizations constitute the outputs.
Figure 3.2.2 illustrates the conceptual framework.
3.2.3 Operational Framework
The operational framework describes the step-by-step execution of the system. The user captures an image through the Android camera flow or selects one from the gallery. The image is preprocessed through resize/letterboxing, normalization, and EXIF orientation correction before being passed to the bundled TensorFlow Lite detector for inference. The output is post-processed with confidence filtering, coordinate remapping, and Dart-side Non-Maximum Suppression to produce bounding boxes, labels, and counts.
Spatial data is gathered from one or more available sources: current GPS, last-known GPS, EXIF GPS, or manual map-based pinning. The geofence service then performs a local point-in-polygon check against field boundaries stored in SQLite. The resulting record is saved locally to the captured-photo history, and a pending cloud operation may be placed in the upload queue. When the user is signed in and connectivity is available, the synchronization service uploads eligible items to Supabase and links the local row to its remote identifier and image URL. After sign-in, previously uploaded rows may be restored into the local gallery even when the original local file is no longer present. The system does not implement continuous still-image detection; all detection is performed on discrete still images captured or selected by the user.

3.3.1 Technology Background
PINYA-PIC was developed as a local-first Android application using Flutter and Dart. Its Android configuration uses compileSdk 36, targetSdk 36, and minSdk 24, with Java 21, Kotlin 2.1.0, Android Gradle Plugin 8.10.0, and Gradle 8.12. The mobile application loads a bundled TensorFlow Lite detector, performs on-device preprocessing and post-processing, and stores records locally before cloud upload.

Location services use geolocator (^11.0.0), mapping uses flutter_map (^6.1.0), biometric authentication uses local_auth (^2.1.7), and connectivity awareness uses connectivity_plus (^6.1.0). The application supports local-first operation: captured results are stored locally first, and cloud synchronization is attempted only when the user is authenticated and connectivity checks succeed.
3.3.2 Software Stack
3.3.2.1 Model Training and Export
3.3.2.1.1 YOLO26 Architecture (Ultralytics YOLO26n) – The final deployed model is based on Ultralytics YOLO26n (Nano variant). Training and retraining are performed using a custom script (retrain_yolo.py), with the ability to switch backbone weights via the --weights parameter. The YOLO11-based metrics represent archived benchmarking results, while the deployed mobile application uses the YOLO26n model optimized for on-device inference.
The default configuration uses yolo26n.pt. The trained model is exported to TensorFlow Lite (best.tflite) with non-maximum suppression disabled (nms=False), allowing post-processing to be handled within the mobile application.
3.3.2.1.2 Dataset Preparation – The dataset was obtained from Roboflow (workspace pine3, project mealybug-y4fsp, version 5). The export contained 2,328 training images and 665 validation images. Annotations included bounding boxes; segmentation masks were present but ignored for detection training.
3.3.2.1.3 Training Configuration – Training was run in two legs due to VRAM constraints. The first leg used 50 epochs, the second leg continued to a total of 100 epochs. Key parameters: input size 640×640, batch size 1, AMP enabled, patience 15, plots disabled. Ultralytics 8.4.21 was used with custom patches to prevent validation batch doubling and to disable rectangular validation when VRAM is limited. After a completed leg, the checkpoint optimizer is stripped; a helper function modifies the checkpoint’s train_args["epochs"] to allow resuming with a higher target epoch count.
3.3.2.1.4 Model Export to TensorFlow Lite – Project documentation describes export to TensorFlow Lite with graph-level NMS disabled so that Non-Maximum Suppression can be handled in Dart for mobile compatibility.
3.3.2.1.5 Non Maximum Suppression in Dart – NMS is implemented in InferenceService, together with confidence filtering, coordinate remapping, and optional higher-recall accuracy mode.

Severity Score and Map Markers: A new utility severity_score.dart computes severity as s=1-e^(-(b⋅c/100)/8). Map markers are colored from green (low severity) to red (high severity) with corresponding glow intensity. An optional grid overlay averages severity per cell.
3.4 Testing
3.4.1 Qualifying Parameters for Usability Testing
Participants for usability testing were independent pineapple farmers from Polomolok, South Cotabato, with a minimum of five years of farming experience and direct experience with mealybug infestations. Twelve participants were recruited, representing a range of ages, farming experience, and technology comfort levels (basic, intermediate, advanced). All participants used Android smartphones.
For the quantitative System Usability Scale (SUS) analysis, only 10 valid completed questionnaires were included in score computation.
3.4.2 System Usability Scale Testing Framework
The System Usability Scale (SUS) was employed to evaluate the perceived usability of the PINYA-PIC application. The SUS is a standardized, ten-item questionnaire with five-point Likert scale responses (1 = strongly disagree to 5 = strongly agree). Its simplicity and robustness make it ideal for gathering feedback from farmers with limited technical experience.
3.4.3 Usability Testing Method
3.4.3.1 Task-Based Testing Protocol
Participants were asked to complete a set of tasks that covered core functionality: create a new user account; log in with biometric authentication; create a new field with preview image; edit an existing field using the current field-edit action; capture a photo and detect mealybugs; interpret the trend chart in the Diagnose tab; submit feedback; test offline mode by saving detections locally; reconnect and verify synchronization; enable device unlock and verify the next-launch prompt; toggle Filipino language and observe UI changes; view the saved-images gallery; and simulate reinstall plus account-linked restoration.
This volume of detections within a short deployment period demonstrates the system’s practical applicability and its capability to support continuous field monitoring.
3.4.3.2 Questionnaire Design
The SUS questionnaire comprised the ten standard items. Task specific questions were also asked to assess feature specific usability, such as ease of capturing and saving a detection, understanding of the chart, perception of offline functionality, ease of enabling device unlock, usefulness of the severity map, and clarity of the online required dialog.
3.4.3.3 SUS Score Calculation
SUS scores were calculated using the standard formula: for odd numbered items, subtract 1 from the response value; for even numbered items, subtract the response value from 5. Sum these adjusted values and multiply by 2.5 to obtain a score between 0 and 100.
3.4.3.4 Interpreting the SUS Score
A score of 100 represents perfect usability; the industry average is 68. Scores above 70 are considered good, above 80 excellent, and below 50 indicate serious usability issues.
3.4.3.5 Qualitative Feedback Collection
After completing the SUS questionnaire, participants were asked open-ended questions about what they liked most, what was difficult or confusing, what features they would like to see added, and whether they would recommend the application to other farmers.
3.4.4 Functional Testing
Functional testing verified that the implemented features performed according to specification. Test cases covered user registration and login, field management, image capture and gallery import, mealybug detection, geotagging and local boundary checks, offline operation, online synchronization, feedback submission, profile management, chart rendering, network-aware dialogs, device unlock, Filipino toggle, saved-images gallery behavior, cloud restoration after reinstall, per-detection confidence display, severity map markers, and split-ABI Android build verification.

3.4.5 Performance Testing
Performance testing measured inference time on two device classes (mid range and budget Android devices), database query times, and sync throughput. Inference times averaged 150 ms on a mid range device and 280 ms on a budget device, meeting the target of under 300 ms. SQLite queries executed in under 50 ms. Sync processing averaged 100 ms per record. Split APK builds reduced per device download size from ~85 MB to approximately 29 36 MB per ABI, a reduction of over 60% compared to a single universal APK.
3.4.6 Offline Mode Testing
Offline mode was tested by disabling Wi-Fi and mobile data, capturing detections, and verifying that records were saved locally and inserted into the upload queue. After re-enabling connectivity, synchronization was triggered through the application’s synchronization pathways, and previously uploaded detections became available again through the saved-images gallery after reinstall and sign-in.
3.4.7 Security Testing
Security testing verified that Supabase RLS policies enforced user isolation: users could only access their own records, and administrator users had elevated privileges. Authentication tokens were properly managed, and biometric authentication functioned as intended. No sensitive data was stored in plain text; API keys were restricted to the Android application via package name and SHA-1 fingerprint. Device unlock was tested by enabling the feature, logging out, and verifying that the unlock screen appeared on the next launch; failure to authenticate correctly prevented access to the main application.

CHAPTER IV
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
4.0 System Features and Functionalities
The PINYA-PIC mobile application was successfully implemented as a local-first, Android-based tool for documenting and reviewing mealybug infestations in pineapple plantations. The application is organized around authentication and onboarding, dashboard navigation, field management, still-image detection, saved-image history, map-based review, profile and settings flows, and cloud-backed restoration of uploaded detections. This section describes the implemented behavior of the system.
4.0.1 Authentication and Onboarding
The authentication and onboarding subsystem provides a staged first-use flow. On first launch, users accept the terms and privacy requirements. After that, the application presents an introduction flow and welcome screen before registration or login. Registration and login are backed by Supabase Auth. After a successful sign-in, users without a display name are redirected to the nickname prompt, and users may also be shown an opt-in prompt for device unlock.
Device unlock is implemented as an optional post-login protection layer. Once enabled, the application requires biometric or device-credential authentication before the user can access the main dashboard on a new app run. This behavior can later be changed in Settings.
4.0.2 Dashboard and Navigation
After sign-in, the user enters the Main Dashboard. The bottom navigation contains four content tabs—Home, Diagnose, My Fields, and More—plus a central Scan action that opens the photo source picker. This means the user sees five navigation items, but the central camera button is an action rather than a persistent content page.
The Home tab includes greeting text, a recent saved-images strip, a horizontal field list, and a non-editable map preview. The Diagnose tab displays summary statistics and a 7-day line chart of detection counts. The My Fields tab provides field cards and a Reminders sub-tab. The More tab provides access to educational content, settings, support pages, and related screens.
4.0.3 Field and Plot Management
The implemented system is field-focused rather than plot-focused. Users can create a field, add a preview image, store an address label, and define or revise the field boundary using the map-based boundary editor. They can also edit existing fields from explicit edit buttons or menu actions in the interface. The application does not implement a separate active plot data model.
Field-related media is handled through the field detail and manage-field-photos flows. Recent detections can also be assigned to a field after capture, allowing the gallery and dashboard to remain useful even when a user did not select a field before scanning.
4.0.4 Camera and Detection Pipeline
The detection pipeline is still-image based. When the user taps Scan, the application opens a source picker that uses the Android camera flow or gallery selection rather than a continuous live frame stream. For camera capture, the application requests a still image and then runs inference on the saved bytes. For gallery images, the application can optionally ask where the photo was taken so that the user can pin the location on a map when the original image lacks GPS metadata.
After image acquisition, the application preprocesses the image through EXIF-aware orientation handling, resize/letterboxing to 640×640, normalization, and coordinate transform tracking. The bundled TensorFlow Lite detector is then executed within the inference service. The output is parsed, confidence-filtered, and processed with Dart-side Non-Maximum Suppression. The resulting detection set includes bounding boxes, count, and per-detection confidence values that are later displayed on result and detail screens.
Spatial metadata is collected from available sources such as current GPS, last-known GPS, EXIF GPS, or manual map pins. The geofence service then performs a local point-in-polygon check against stored field boundaries. The finished result is saved locally to the captured-photo history and, when appropriate, inserted into the upload queue for later cloud synchronization.
4.0.5 Geospatial Services
Geospatial features provide spatial context for saved detections. Field boundaries are stored locally and can be mirrored to the cloud through the boundary_json field of the fields table. The map experience includes a preview map on the Home tab, a dedicated detections map screen, boundary-aware location selection, and severity-based marker rendering.
The application’s geofencing is implemented as local point-in-polygon logic rather than background operating-system geofence triggers. This means the system checks whether a coordinate falls inside a known field boundary when needed for validation, display, and field association, but it does not continuously monitor entry and exit events in the background.
The detections map also supports severity-based visualization. Severity is derived from bug count and confidence and is mapped to color and glow intensity so that users can visually identify more serious areas of concern.
4.0.6 Offline Synchronization
The application follows a local-first workflow. New captures are stored locally before cloud upload. SQLite keeps the field boundary data, saved-image history, pending upload queue, and cached field metadata needed for offline use. When the device is offline, users can continue creating local records and reviewing previously saved content.
When the user is signed in and connectivity becomes available, the synchronization service attempts to upload pending records to Supabase. The upload path links local rows to remote detection identifiers and image URLs after successful upload. Synchronization is triggered through save, refresh, and dashboard-entry paths rather than through an always-running background connectivity listener.
Account-linked restoration complements offline synchronization. After sign-in, previously uploaded detections can be pulled from Supabase into the local gallery. Only records that already reached the cloud can be restored; purely offline, unsynchronized detections remain local-only and are lost after reinstallation.
4.0.7 Analytics and Visualization
The Diagnose tab summarizes recent activity through three headline statistics and a 7-day trend chart. The statistics report image count, field count, and infestation rate over the recent period. The trend chart uses a smooth-curve presentation with peak highlighting to improve readability for non-technical users.
The dashboard can use either cloud-backed or local data depending on current availability. This design helps the application remain useful even when connectivity is unreliable.
Figure 4.6 shows the Diagnose tab with sample data; the peak-highlighted chart is highlighted.
4.0.8 Feedback System
The feedback system includes two implemented paths. The first is email feedback, which opens a mail client when available and falls back to a copy-to-clipboard dialog when no mail handler is present. The second is an in-app feedback form that submits JSON payloads to a web endpoint. Both flows are guarded by online checks when needed.
This design provides a more resilient support experience than relying on only one feedback channel. If a device cannot launch a mail app, the user can still copy the destination email address. If the user prefers structured submission, the in-app form provides a direct path to the configured endpoint.
4.0.9 Profile Management
The Profile page allows the user to manage display name, email, phone number, and avatar. Profile images are uploaded to Supabase Storage, and a cache-busting parameter is appended to the URL so updated images appear immediately. The page also shows profile-related status information and supports editing without leaving the application.
4.0.10 Network Awareness
Network awareness is handled through both user-interface and background-synchronization checks. For user-interface flows, the application verifies that a usable network interface exists before attempting actions such as opening online-only pages or submitting web-based feedback. For background synchronization, the application uses a stricter reachability check so that synchronization proceeds only when internet access appears genuinely available.
This distinction improves usability by avoiding unnecessary blocking of UI flows when direct DNS checks fail but ordinary app traffic may still work.
4.0.11 Saved Images Gallery and Account-Linked Restoration
The saved-images experience is one of the application’s central features. The Home tab shows recent captures, while the dedicated captured-photos flows provide expanded review, detail viewing, and assign-to-field actions. Each saved row can include image path, count, confidence, field association, location, and remote linkage.
After a reinstall, the application can restore already uploaded detections by inserting remotely linked rows into the local gallery. In this case, the interface may display the remote image URL when the original local file is missing. A current limitation is that per-box detection data are not restored from the server, so restored rows may lack the full historical overlay detail available in the original local capture.

4.3.5 Comparison with Validation Metrics
The real‑world average confidence of 33.2% is consistent with the validation mAP@0.5 of 0.526 reported in Section 4.1. While mAP measures bounding‑box overlap, the confidence score correlates with detection certainty; the moderate values reflect the inherent difficulty of detecting small, clustered mealybugs. Notably, the fields with the highest average confidence (George and Moso, 37.7%) also had the highest mealybug counts (490 each), suggesting that the model performs better when infestations are dense – a useful insight for farmers.
4.4 Summary of Results
The PINYA-PIC mobile application was successfully implemented and evaluated as a functional system for real-world agricultural use. Training documentation reported validation metrics of mAP@0.5 = 0.526, precision = 0.667, and recall = 0.462, while the deployed application utilizes a bundled YOLO26n TensorFlow Lite detector for on-device inference. The local-first architecture, supported by SQLite and Supabase synchronization, was validated through both functional and offline-mode testing.
Additional system enhancements—such as network reachability, online-required dialogs, coordinate transformation, per-detection confidence visualization, device unlock, split APK builds, account-linked gallery, severity mapping, and Filipino language support—contributed to improved system reliability, usability, and performance. The cloud restoration mechanism further ensures that previously uploaded detection records remain accessible across device changes and reinstalls, although this is limited to synchronized data.
A total of 12 farmers participated in usability evaluation, with 10 valid responses included in the System Usability Scale (SUS) analysis, yielding a mean score of 77.0. All usability tasks were completed successfully, and qualitative feedback emphasized the importance of offline capability, intuitive field management, clear visualization tools, and robust feedback mechanisms.
Real-world deployment over a three-day period resulted in 826 detection operations, identifying 4,670 mealybugs across 12 fields with an average confidence score of 33.2%. These results demonstrate that the system operates reliably under actual farming conditions and provides actionable pest detection insights.
Overall, the findings confirm that PINYA-PIC is effective in identifying mealybug infestations and supporting early detection and targeted intervention. The integration of offline functionality, geospatial mapping, and accessible visualization makes the system practical for farmers, particularly in environments with limited connectivity.
CHAPTER V
CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
5.0 Conclusion
This study demonstrates that a mobile-based, machine learning-driven system can effectively support agricultural pest monitoring under real-world conditions, particularly in environments with limited connectivity. The successful implementation and deployment of PINYA-PIC confirm its practicality as a tool for assisting pineapple farmers in identifying and managing mealybug infestations.
By enabling early detection and spatial tracking of infestations, the system supports more informed decision-making and targeted intervention strategies. These capabilities contribute to improved pest management efficiency and have the potential to reduce crop losses while promoting more sustainable farming practices.
Despite these contributions, the system has several limitations, including moderate detection confidence due to the small size and clustering of mealybugs, dependence on dataset quality, and reliance on successful cloud synchronization for complete data restoration. These limitations present opportunities for further refinement and future enhancement of the system.
5.0.1 Summary of Contributions
This study successfully developed PINYA-PIC, a machine learning-driven mobile application for detecting and monitoring mealybug infestations in pineapple plantations. The system integrates on-device detection, geospatial data handling, and offline-first architecture to provide a practical support tool for agricultural monitoring.
Key contributions of the study include:
AI Model Integration: The integration of a YOLO26n TensorFlow Lite detector enables efficient on-device inference, achieving acceptable performance for mobile deployment with inference times ranging from approximately 150–280 ms.
Offline-First Architecture: The implementation of SQLite as the primary local store, combined with a synchronization mechanism using Supabase, ensures system functionality even in environments with unreliable internet connectivity.
Field Management System: The system allows direct editing of field records, reducing user effort and improving data management efficiency.
Visualization and Analytics: The application provides temporal and spatial visualization tools, including trend charts and severity-based map overlays, to support interpretation of infestation patterns.
Robust System Design: Features such as network reachability checks, coordinate transformation, error handling, and feedback mechanisms enhance system reliability.
Usability Validation: The usability evaluation yielded a mean SUS score of 77.0, indicating above-average usability, with users successfully completing all tasks and providing positive qualitative feedback.
Field Deployment Validation: Real-world testing recorded 826 detections and 4,670 identified mealybugs across 12 fields, demonstrating consistent system performance in actual farming conditions.
5.0.2 Achievement of Objectives
All objectives outlined in the study were successfully achieved and validated through both functional testing and real-world deployment. The integration of the YOLO26n model, mobile application development, geospatial features, and offline synchronization capabilities were implemented as intended.
Beyond technical completion, the system was evaluated in real farming environments, demonstrating its practical effectiveness in supporting pest monitoring and management. These results confirm that the study achieved its goals not only in design and development but also in real-world application.
5.0.3 Technical Contributions
This study contributes to the field by demonstrating how mobile-based artificial intelligence can be effectively implemented in agricultural environments:
Offline-Capable AI Systems: The combination of local storage and cloud synchronization provides a practical model for deploying intelligent systems in low-connectivity environments.
Optimized Mobile Detection Pipeline: The use of a lightweight YOLO26n TensorFlow Lite model with integrated preprocessing and post-processing demonstrates feasibility for on-device pest detection.
Farmer-Centered UX Design: The system incorporates design elements tailored for users with varying levels of technical experience, including clear visualizations and simplified workflows.
Resilient Mobile Application Features: The inclusion of network-aware dialogs, fallback mechanisms, device authentication, and language support improves system reliability and accessibility.
5.0.4 Usability Findings
The usability evaluation highlights several important findings. Participants reported that offline functionality is essential for field use, as connectivity is often limited in agricultural areas. The system’s ability to operate without internet access was consistently valued by users.
The interface design supported ease of use, with participants able to perform all tasks successfully. Visualization tools, such as trend charts and severity maps, improved users’ understanding of infestation patterns, while clear error messages increased system transparency.
A moderate correlation between technology familiarity and usability scores indicates that additional onboarding support may enhance accessibility for users with limited experience. Overall, the results confirm that the system is usable, understandable, and effective for the target users.
5.1 Recommendations
5.1.1 Immediate Improvements
Based on the findings of this study and the analysis of real world detection data, the following immediate improvements are recommended:
Feedback Endpoint Hardening: Maintain and document the current web-backed feedback endpoint, and provide an administrative procedure for updating it if the endpoint changes in the future.
Onboarding Tutorial: Add an interactive tutorial for first time users highlighting key features such as field editing, offline mode, chart interpretation, device unlock, severity map, and the saved images gallery. This would address the correlation between technology comfort and SUS scores.
Simplified Language: Review technical terms (e.g., "confidence score," "severity," "geofencing") and replace them with farmer friendly alternatives where possible, or add tooltips with explanations.
Larger Touch Targets: Increase the touch target sizes for interactive elements (especially field cards and buttons) to accommodate users with limited fine motor control or those using the app in field conditions.
Complete Filipino Translations: Finish translating all disease descriptions and UI strings to Filipino for users who prefer that language.
Confidence Score Explanation: Add a simple indicator (e.g., low/medium/high) next to the numerical confidence score, because some farmers may not intuitively understand a percentage. For instance, <30% = "Low certainty, re-check", 30–60% = "Moderate certainty", and >60% = "High certainty".
These recommendations provide a structured pathway for improving the system’s accuracy, usability, and scalability, supporting its transition from a research prototype into a more robust and deployable agricultural solution.
5.1.2 Future Enhancements
The following enhancements are recommended for future versions of PINYA-PIC:
Multiple Disease Detection: Extend the deployed YOLO26n detector to identify additional pineapple pests and diseases (e.g., black heart rot, wilt disease) by collecting and annotating a larger multi-class dataset.
Offline Maps: Implement downloadable map tiles so that the map functionality works completely offline, which is particularly valuable in remote areas with poor or no cellular coverage.
Multi Language Support: Add support for additional local languages such as Cebuano and Hiligaynon to improve accessibility for farmers who do not speak Filipino or English fluently.
Export Reports: Add PDF and CSV export functionality for infestation reports, enabling farmers to share data with agricultural extension workers or keep printed records.
Weather Integration: Integrate weather data (temperature, humidity, rainfall) to analyze correlations between environmental conditions and infestation patterns, potentially enabling predictive alerts.
Sync Queue Management UI: Provide users with visibility into pending sync operations (e.g., a list of unsynced detections with retry status) and the ability to manually trigger sync or clear failed items.
Predictive Analytics: Develop predictive models that forecast infestation risk based on historical detection data, weather patterns, and seasonal factors, providing proactive recommendations.
Tablet Optimization: Adapt the user interface for tablet devices to support larger screen sizes common in extension offices and training centers.
Store Per Box JSON on Server: Enhance the cloud restoration feature by storing the full per detection confidence JSON on the server, so that cloud restored rows can display individual bounding box confidence labels, not just overall counts.
Model Retraining with Field Data: Use the real-world detections and verified labels gathered during field deployment to fine-tune the deployed YOLO26n model, potentially improving confidence scores and reducing false negatives.
5.1.3 Research Directions
The following research directions are proposed based on the findings and limitations of this study:
Longitudinal Usability Study: Conduct a longer term study (e.g., 3 6 months) to assess how usability perceptions evolve with sustained use of the application, and to measure actual impact on pest management practices.
Impact Assessment: Evaluate the economic and environmental impact of the application on pesticide use, crop yields, and farmer incomes through controlled field trials and cost benefit analysis.
Cross Cultural Validation: Validate the usability findings with farmers in different regions of the Philippines (e.g., Bukidnon, Davao) and in other pineapple producing countries to assess generalizability.
Model Transfer Learning: Investigate transfer learning approaches to adapt the YOLO26n detector for other crops and pests with minimal additional training data, potentially using few-shot learning techniques.
Test Set Benchmarking: Perform a separate evaluation on a held out test set (not used during training or validation) to establish a more reliable estimate of real world model performance, and report those metrics alongside the validation numbers.
