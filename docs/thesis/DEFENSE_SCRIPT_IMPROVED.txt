PART 1: TITLE
─────────────

"Good day, esteemed members of the panel, our advisor, and guests. I am
Gename Bacay, and together with my co-proponents, Angelei Morillo and
Janrafael Morga, we are pleased to present our final defense for our study
titled:

PINYA-PIC: A Machine Learning-Driven Mobile Application for Mealybug
Infestation Detection with Integrated Geotagging and Geofencing Features.

This study was conceptualized and conducted at Mindanao State University–
General Santos, aimed at addressing pressing technological gaps in precision
agriculture for pineapple farming in Region XII."


PART 2: BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT
──────────────────────────────

"To understand the motivation behind this study, we must look at the
current state of pineapple production.

The Philippines is a major pineapple exporter, and South Cotabato is one of
the country's key production areas. However, yields are constantly
threatened by pests — most notably, mealybugs. These are invasive,
sap-sucking insects that cause severe crop damage, stunt plant growth, and
introduce pineapple mealybug wilt-associated viruses.

The critical challenge is detection. Mealybugs are extremely small — often
just 2 to 4 millimeters — and they hide deep within the crevices of the
plant's leaves. They cluster together and are covered in a white, waxy
coating that makes them difficult to distinguish from plant tissue. Because
they multiply rapidly, if an infestation is not caught early, it spreads
across entire fields within weeks, leading to significant financial losses
for farmers.

Currently, farmers rely entirely on manual visual inspection — a process
that is slow, labor-intensive, and prone to human error, especially for
pests this small."


PART 3: PROBLEM STATEMENT
─────────────────────────

"This study addresses three key problems:

First, there is a lack of real-time pest detection tools available to
small-scale pineapple farmers. Existing agricultural AI solutions target
general crop diseases — none specifically addresses mealybug detection in
pineapples.

Second, existing systems lack location-based monitoring. Without geospatial
tracking, farmers cannot map where infestations occur or monitor how they
spread over time.

Third, current detection relies on manual inspection, which is slow, prone
to error, and cannot provide quantified data for decision-making.

These challenges highlight the need for an automated, mobile, and
location-aware detection solution."


PART 4: GENERAL OBJECTIVES
──────────────────────────

"Our study has three main objectives:

First — to develop and integrate a YOLO26n-based object detection model,
deployed in TensorFlow Lite format, for real-time on-device detection of
mealybug presence and population density.

Second — to build a field-focused Android mobile application using Flutter,
with offline-first architecture, that integrates GPS geotagging,
point-in-polygon geofencing, historical data visualization, and severity-
based analytics for farmers.

Third — to evaluate the system's usability through the System Usability
Scale framework with actual pineapple farmers in Polomolok, South Cotabato."


PART 5: SIGNIFICANCE OF THE STUDY
──────────────────────────────────

"This study is significant for several reasons:

For farmers, it provides an accessible tool for early pest detection —
reducing reliance on manual inspection and enabling faster response to
infestations.

For agricultural agencies, the system generates geospatial data and
severity maps that can inform region-wide pest monitoring strategies.

For researchers, this study contributes to the growing body of literature
on lightweight AI models for precision agriculture in developing-country
contexts.

The system also promotes sustainable agriculture by enabling targeted
intervention — potentially reducing blanket pesticide application — and
aligns with Sustainable Development Goals: Zero Hunger, Industry Innovation
and Infrastructure, Responsible Consumption and Production, and Life on
Land."


PART 6: SCOPE
─────────────

"The scope of this study covers four areas:

AI Model Development — we trained a YOLO26n object detection model on over
19,000 Roboflow-managed images and optimized it into a 5.42-megabyte
TensorFlow Lite model for real-time, on-device inference.

Mobile and Data Infrastructure — we developed an offline-first Flutter
application with SQLite for local storage and Supabase for cloud
synchronization when internet is available.

Geospatial and Field Analytics — we implemented a ray-casting algorithm for
point-in-polygon geofencing, GPS/EXIF geotagging, and OpenStreetMap-based
visualization with interactive trend charts.

Usability Assessment — we evaluated the application using the SUS framework
through hands-on testing with 10 local pineapple farmers."


PART 7: SYSTEM LIMITATIONS
──────────────────────────

"We acknowledge the following limitations:

Geographic and Pest Constraints — the system is confined strictly to
mealybug detection within pineapple plantations in Polomolok, South
Cotabato. It is not designed for other pest types or crop varieties.

Technical Dependencies — detection performance relies on device-specific
hardware capabilities. Budget devices may experience slightly longer
inference times, up to 280 milliseconds per image. GPS accuracy depends
on the device's positioning hardware.

Data Recovery Limits — cloud restoration is restricted to fully
synchronized items. Bounding-box confidence scores for individual
detections are stored locally and cannot be recovered after app
reinstallation if not previously synced.

I will now hand over to Angelei, who will discuss our literature review,
methodology, and system design."

PART 8: LITERATURE REVIEW
─────────────────────────

"Thank you, Gena.

Our literature review examined existing research across three domains:

In Machine Learning and Deep Learning Evolution — we found that
agricultural pest detection has advanced from traditional Convolutional
Neural Networks to real-time object detection models. Studies by Li et al.
(2022) and Chen et al. (2021) demonstrate that YOLO-based architectures
achieve the best balance of speed and accuracy for pest detection tasks.

In terms of The YOLO Advantage — YOLO models enable single-pass detection,
making them suitable for resource-constrained mobile environments. The
YOLO26n variant, released in 2025, offers architectural improvements over
previous versions while maintaining a nano-scale model size optimized for
edge deployment.

In Geospatial and Architectural Integration — prior research shows that
combining mobile photo capture with geotagging enhances agricultural data
tracking (Soni et al., 2021), while offline-first local architectures using
SQLite sync queues provide system resilience in remote field environments
where cellular connectivity is unreliable.

In Validated Usability Frameworks — the System Usability Scale, established
by Brooke (1996) and benchmarked by Sauro (2011), provides a reliable and
standardized instrument for evaluating system acceptability with non-
technical users."


PART 9: RELATED TECHNOLOGY
──────────────────────────

"We compared PINYA-PIC against four existing agricultural technology
platforms: Agrio, Cropx, Plantix, and SPIDTECH.

As shown in our comparison table, while these platforms offer pest
detection or agricultural monitoring individually, none provides all of the
following: mealybug-specific detection, mealybug population counting,
geotagging, geofencing, targeted intervention mapping, hybrid monitoring
with offline capability, and sustainability-focused features — all in a
single application.

PINYA-PIC is the only system that checks every feature except multi-pest
detection — which is intentionally outside our scope, as we focused on
specialization rather than generalization."


PART 10: METHODOLOGY
────────────────────

"Our development followed a Modified Waterfall methodology, structured into
five phases:

Requirements Gathering — we conducted field interviews with 15 pineapple
farmers in Polomolok, South Cotabato, to identify operational pain points
and translate them into user stories.

Design — we established 39 high-fidelity mobile screen mockups and 13
structural activity diagrams to define the system architecture.

Implementation — we built the mobile application in Flutter across 19
service layers, integrating the YOLO26n TFLite model for offline detection,
a ray-casting algorithm for geofencing, and Supabase with Row-Level Security
for cloud synchronization.

Testing — we employed a dual-track quality assurance strategy combining
laboratory functional testing with end-to-end field trials involving 10
local farmers, validating the system's offline functionality and data
visualizations.

Deployment — we compiled architecture-specific Android APKs, optimized at
29 to 36 megabytes for entry-level devices, and transitioned the system to
long-term maintenance."


PART 11: SYSTEM FEATURES
─────────────────────────

"The core features of PINYA-PIC include:

Detection — users capture an image of a pineapple plant, and the app
instantly processes it through the on-device YOLO26n model, displaying
bounding boxes around detected mealybugs with confidence scores and a total
count.

Geospatial Tracking — each detection is automatically tagged with GPS
coordinates, and the app determines whether the detection falls within a
registered pineapple field using geofencing.

Field Management — farmers can register field boundaries, edit field
details, and view detection history for each field.

Analytics Dashboard — the app provides severity maps, trend charts with
peak highlighting, and field-level statistics to help farmers understand
infestation patterns over time.

Offline-First Architecture — all features work without internet. Data
synchronizes to the cloud automatically when connectivity is restored."


PART 12: TESTING APPROACH
─────────────────────────

"The system underwent four types of testing:

Functional Testing — verifying that each feature operates as specified.

Integration Testing — confirming that components work together: detection
pipeline, geolocation services, database operations, and cloud
synchronization.

Performance Testing — measuring inference speed across device tiers:
approximately 150 milliseconds on mid-range devices and 280 milliseconds
on budget devices.

User Acceptance Testing — where three Department of Agriculture experts
independently validated detection correctness on 21 field images, and 10
farmers evaluated the application's usability using the SUS framework.

I will now hand over to Janrafael for our results and discussion."

PART 13: MODEL PERFORMANCE RESULTS
───────────────────────────────────

"Thank you, Angelei.

Let me now present our results, beginning with model performance.

We evaluated the model through two complementary approaches:

First — automated benchmark testing. On a held-out test set of 1,952
labeled images, the model achieved 72.8% precision, 58.9% recall, and an
mAP@0.5 of 61.0%. The mAP@0.5:0.95 — which is a stricter metric — was
29.2%. These are evaluated at the academic standard confidence threshold of
0.12.

To put this in context: mealybugs are extremely small objects, often under
20 pixels in an image. Published benchmarks for small-pest detection using
nano-sized models report 45 to 65% mAP at 0.5. Our 61% places us at the
upper end of that range.

Second — manual expert validation. Three validators from the Office of the
Municipal Agriculturist independently reviewed 21 field images — 7 each —
at the deployed 30% confidence threshold. Results showed:

  Precision: 99.58%
  Recall: 85.64%
  F1 Score: 91.75%
  Accuracy: 85.40%

The key takeaway: what the model detects is almost always correct. The
99.58% precision means only 1 false positive was observed across all 122
ground-truth mealybug instances.

These two evaluations measure different things: mAP evaluates bounding-box
overlap across all confidence levels, while expert validation evaluates
practical correctness at our deploy threshold. Both confirm the system is
effective for its intended purpose."


PART 14: MODEL EVOLUTION
────────────────────────

"To demonstrate improvement, we evaluated all model versions on the same
fixed 462-image test set:

  v2  — our initial model — achieved just 21.1% mAP.
  v10 — after scaling to 16,000 images — reached 42.1%.
  v11 — after label cleaning — 43.0%.
  v12 — trained at higher resolution — 43.8%.
  v13afix — our final deployed model — 56.7%.

This represents a total improvement of 35.6 percentage points from our
baseline. The largest single gain, 12.9 points from v12 to v13afix, came
from incorporating real field data collected during deployment, additional
augmentation, and systematic label cleaning."


PART 15: FIELD DEPLOYMENT RESULTS
─────────────────────────────────

"During our three-day field deployment in Polomolok, the system processed
826 detection operations and identified a total of 4,670 mealybugs across
12 registered pineapple fields.

The average detection confidence was 33.2%. We want to clarify what this
means: our threshold is 30%, so the system displays all detections at 30%
and above. Many mealybugs are tiny and partially occluded, resulting in
confidence scores in the 30 to 50% range. However, our expert validation
confirmed that even at these confidence levels, precision remains at
99.58% — meaning low confidence does not mean incorrect detection. It
simply reflects the inherent difficulty of detecting very small, clustered
insects."


PART 16: USABILITY RESULTS
───────────────────────────

"For usability evaluation, 10 pineapple farmers completed the System
Usability Scale questionnaire immediately after hands-on field testing.

Results:
  Farmer SUS Score: 77.0 out of 100 — rated 'Good,' above the industry
  benchmark of 68.

Additionally, administrators evaluated the system's management interface
and dashboard, achieving a SUS score of 82.5 — rated 'Excellent.'

Qualitative feedback highlighted three key strengths:

  First — the offline functionality. Farmers emphasized that being able to
  capture pest data and save photos without cellular network — and sync to
  the cloud later — is essential for remote field environments.

  Second — field editing. Farmers appreciated being able to correct field
  names and boundaries directly within the interface without recreating
  entire records.

  Third — the severity map and visual analytics. Farmers found the color-
  coded severity maps and peak-highlighting charts highly intuitive,
  noting that visual trends were far easier to comprehend and act upon
  compared to raw numbers."


PART 17: CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS
───────────────────────────────────────

"In conclusion, this study successfully developed and deployed a machine
learning-driven mobile application for mealybug infestation detection.

Our key contributions are:

  One — a specialized YOLO26n detection model achieving 61.0% mAP@0.5
  on labeled benchmarks and 91.75% F1 score in expert field validation,
  packaged in a 5.42-megabyte TFLite model suitable for budget devices.

  Two — a complete offline-first mobile system integrating detection,
  geotagging, geofencing, and severity analytics — validated through 826
  field detection operations across 12 farms over three days.

  Three — demonstrated usability with SUS scores of 77.0 for farmers and
  82.5 for administrators, both exceeding the industry benchmark of 68.

For future work, we recommend:

  Expanding the training dataset with more field-collected images to
  improve recall.

  Testing larger YOLO variants for higher accuracy where device
  constraints permit.

  Extending the system to cover additional pest types and crop varieties.

  Conducting a larger-scale longitudinal study to measure long-term
  impact on farmer decision-making.

Thank you. We are now open for questions from the panel."
